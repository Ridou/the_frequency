## THE SCENE

### BEFORE THE ATTACK

Nadia Kaine stood in the shadows outside the Shwedagon Pagoda perimeter, watching 25,000 people arrange themselves in geometric pattern around the golden spire.

This should feel wrong. It didn't.

She checked her tactical display—holographic overlay showing frequency signatures, awakener positions, probability vectors Marcus had calculated. Everything said this operation was clean. Surgical. Necessary.

*Eight soldiers saved. Eight lives. I violated forty free wills to do it.*

The math worked then. The math worked now.

"Team Alpha, position report," she said quietly into her comm.

"North perimeter secured. Awaiting order."

"Team Beta?"

"South perimeter. Ready."

"Team Gamma?"

"West side. All clear."

She had fifteen Controllers positioned around the pagoda's perimeter. Frequency dampers ready. Force projection prepped. Nadia herself would lead the etheric assault—the astral plane attack that would disrupt the awakening before it could complete.

Vera's orders were clear: "Stop this. We can't let 25,000 people awaken at once. Myanmar falls, others follow. End it."

Nadia watched the crowd settle into meditation. Watched the frequency start to build—golden light barely visible to normal sight, but blazing on the astral plane like a second sun rising.

She found Christian Rand at the northern pole. Found the woman—Sophia Rivera—at the southern pole. Both glowing with power that made Nadia's tactical assessment algorithms scream warnings.

*Threat Level: Extreme. Recommend immediate intervention.*

"Standby," she murmured to her teams.

On the astral plane—the consciousness level where awakeners could fight with thought itself—Nadia shifted her perception. The physical world faded. The REAL battlefield emerged.

Frequency warfare.

She'd fought here dozens of times. Never lost.

---

### THE ASSAULT

"All teams: Execute."

Physical team activated frequency dampers—devices Marcus designed, tech that disrupted resonance patterns. Should have destabilized the awakening immediately.

Didn't.

The golden light ABSORBED the interference, grew brighter, like feeding solar flares by throwing water at the sun.

"Dampers ineffective," came the report. Static in her ear. "Repeat, dampers are NOT working."

"Switch to projection," Nadia ordered, already moving.

On the etheric plane, she LAUNCHED herself at the north pole—at Christian Rand specifically. If she could disrupt HIS projection, the circuit would break. 25,000 people would stay asleep. Mission complete.

She manifested her force—the ability she'd used in Afghanistan, refined over seven years. Pure pressure, thought made tangible, fear weaponized.

Hit Christian Rand's frequency signature like missile strike.

Should have disrupted him. Should have broken his concentration. Should have worked.

His frequency HELD.

More than held—he didn't even seem to NOTICE.

*What the—*

She tried again. More power this time. Force projection at maximum. Making his worst fears tangible, making doubt feel like drowning.

Nothing.

He was so deeply connected to Source, so completely aligned, that her manipulation had nothing to GRIP. No fear to amplify. No doubt to weaponize.

"Target One unaffected," she reported, already changing tactics. "Switching to Target Two."

Sophia Rivera at the south pole.

Nadia redirected her attack, hitting the woman's frequency with surgical precision. Found the fear—Sophia HAD fear, beautiful vulnerable fear about disappearing, about not being enough, about losing herself.

Nadia PUSHED on that fear. Amplified it. Made it CRUSHING.

For three seconds, Sophia wavered.

Then something happened Nadia had never encountered.

The woman's fear... transformed. Right in front of her. Like alchemy, like magic, like something impossible.

Sophia FELT the fear Nadia pushed at her. Held it. Looked at it. Saw what it was protecting. And transmuted it into fierce compassion for everyone who'd ever felt that same terror.

The fear became FUEL.

And Sophia's frequency BLAZED brighter.

"Target Two unaffected," Nadia said, voice tight. "Repeat, targets are UNAFFECTED. They're not defending. They're just... not vulnerable."

"Fall back," came Vera's voice in her ear. "Extraction, now."

"Negative. I can still—"

"That's an ORDER, Nadia. Extract. Now."

On the physical plane, Nadia watched the golden pillar of light rise from the pagoda into the sky. Watched it reach a point where it would be visible from SATELLITES. Watched 25,000 people awaken simultaneously in a cascade of frequency that rewrote the consciousness field of an entire region.

Mission failed.

For the first time in seven years of operations, she'd failed.

---

### AFTERMATH

Three miles away, in the extraction vehicle, Nadia sat in silence.

Her teams were quiet. Professionals. They didn't comment on the failure. Just waited for her assessment.

"They're stronger than Marcus's models predicted," she said finally.

"The math was off?" one of her operators asked carefully.

"No. The math was RIGHT. For every previous Pattern." Nadia stared at her hands—hands that had killed, threatened, terrified. Hands that had just been rendered useless. "This one's different. He's not defending against attacks. He's just... not vulnerable to them. Can't manipulate someone who has no shadow."

"Everyone has shadow," her second-in-command said.

"Yeah. That's what I thought too." She looked out the window toward the distant golden glow still visible over the city. "I was wrong."

In her earpiece: "Nadia. Report."

Vera's voice. Cool, controlled, dangerous.

"Mission failure," Nadia reported. "25,000 awakened. Myanmar is lost. Recommend we reassess combat approach. Traditional methods ineffective against this level of Source alignment."

"Return to base. We'll debrief."

"Acknowledged."

As the vehicle pulled away, Nadia found herself thinking about Afghanistan. About eight soldiers she'd saved by violating forty free wills. About the choice that had seemed so clear then.

*I did what was necessary. They survived.*

But Christian Rand had faced her attack—the full force of her weaponized fear—and hadn't even stumbled.

Not because he was stronger.

Because he wasn't FIGHTING.

She'd brought a weapon to a dance. Brought force to a field that operated on entirely different principles.

And for the first time in seven years, Nadia wondered:

*What if we're doing this wrong?*

The thought was small. Quiet. Easy to ignore.

She ignored it.

Went back to base. Filed her report. Requested new tactical parameters.

Because soldiers don't question. They adapt and execute.

The doubt was there though.

Planted.

Waiting.

Germinating in soil of the first failure she'd ever experienced.
